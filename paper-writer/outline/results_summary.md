## Source Outputs

The empirical evidence for the paper comes from cached Monte Carlo outputs in `mc_coverage_results/` (corrected MLE, IRLS) and `mc_naive_results/` (naive hard-classification baseline), assembled by the reproducible artifact generator `paper-writer/scripts/generate_simulation_evidence.py`. The naive baseline is generated by `paper-writer/scripts/run_naive_baseline.py`, which regenerates each Monte Carlo replication with the matching seed scheme and estimates regime-specific OLS using the modal classifier prediction with cluster-robust standard errors clustered by driver. The evidence script reads 14,400 corrected parquet files plus 14,400 naive parquet files (153,600 parameter-replication rows each) and produces figures and appendix tables under `paper-writer/results/`.

## Simulation Design

The design is a full factorial Monte Carlo over:

- Latent regimes `R in {2, 4, 10}`.
- Misclassification weight `w in {0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9}`.
- Equicorrelation in regime-specific shocks `rho in {0.0, 0.3, 0.6, 0.9}`.
- 200 replications per cell.
- 200 drivers and 15 periods per replication, giving 3,000 observations per replication.

The misclassification parameter `w` maps into a correct-class probability of `1 - w * (1 - 1/R)`. Thus `w = 0.9` is materially different across regime counts: correct-class probability is 0.55 for `R = 2`, 0.325 for `R = 4`, and 0.19 for `R = 10`.

## Generated Figures

### Figure 1: Coverage landscape

- **File**: `paper-writer/results/figures/figure_1_coverage_landscape.png` and `.pdf`
- **What it shows**: Heatmaps of slope-coefficient 95 percent coverage for analytical Wald intervals and score wild bootstrap intervals, by `R`, `rho`, and misclassification weight.
- **Main result**: For moderate misclassification (`w <= 0.7`), average slope coverage is close to nominal: 0.936 for analytical Wald intervals and 0.959 for score wild bootstrap intervals. Coverage deteriorates sharply only in the most weakly identified high-regime cells.
- **Important caveat**: The hardest cells are `R = 4`, `rho = 0.9`, `w = 0.9` with bootstrap coverage 0.142, and `R = 10`, `rho = 0.9`, `w = 0.9` with bootstrap coverage 0.242. These are near-uninformative multi-regime classifier settings.

### Figure 2: RMSE frontier

- **File**: `paper-writer/results/figures/figure_2_rmse_frontier.png` and `.pdf`
- **What it shows**: Slope RMSE against classifier error probability, with panels for shock correlation and lines for MLE and IRLS across regime counts.
- **Main result**: MLE and IRLS have nearly identical RMSE profiles. For `w <= 0.7`, mean MLE slope RMSE is 0.029 for `R = 2`, 0.041 for `R = 4`, and 0.092 for `R = 10`. RMSE increases sharply when the classifier is nearly uninformative in many-regime designs.
- **Interpretation**: The estimator behaves well when classifier probabilities preserve useful regime information, but many-regime designs require more classification signal.

### Figure 3: Baseline bias distribution

- **File**: `paper-writer/results/figures/figure_3_baseline_bias_distribution.png` and `.pdf`
- **What it shows**: Distribution of MLE slope errors in the baseline `R = 2`, `rho = 0` design across the misclassification grid.
- **Main result**: Baseline slope errors remain centered near zero even as misclassification weight rises. The paper should use this as the visual explanation for why coverage remains strong in the two-regime design.

### Figure 5: Naive baseline vs corrected MLE [NEW]

- **File**: `paper-writer/results/figures/figure_5_naive_vs_corrected.png` and `.pdf`
- **What it shows**: Two-panel comparison of slope bias (panel a) and 95% CI coverage (panel b) for the corrected MLE versus the naive hard-classification baseline, plotted against classifier error probability for `R in {2, 4, 10}`. Lines are averaged over `rho`.
- **Main result**: At low classifier error the two estimators are indistinguishable, but the naive baseline's bias and coverage degrade sharply as classification error grows in the multi-regime designs. For `R = 4`, naive coverage drops to 0.527 at classifier error 0.525 and 0.005 at 0.675, while corrected MLE bootstrap coverage is 0.962 and 0.471 respectively. For `R = 10`, naive coverage drops to 0.236 at classifier error 0.63, where corrected MLE bootstrap coverage is 0.931.
- **Interpretation**: Confusion-matrix correction yields large finite-sample gains over a naive baseline as soon as the classifier is meaningfully imperfect in many-regime designs.

### Figure 4: Computation tradeoffs

- **File**: `paper-writer/results/figures/figure_4_computation_tradeoffs.png` and `.pdf`
- **What it shows**: Average IRLS, MLE, and score wild bootstrap runtime per replication by regime count, plus convergence rate by regime count.
- **Main result**: Convergence remains high: 0.998 for `R = 2`, 0.995 for `R = 4`, and 0.984 for `R = 10`. Average MLE time rises from 0.135 seconds at `R = 2` to 6.315 seconds at `R = 10`, so computation is feasible but scaling is nontrivial.

## Caveats and Flags

The naive hard-classification baseline is now available in `mc_naive_results/` and is integrated into Figure 5, the compact coverage table (`table_A2_coverage_rho_0_6.tex`), and the machine summary. The naive estimator uses the modal classifier prediction and cluster-robust standard errors clustered at the driver level. Two specification choices to flag for the paper write-up: (i) when fewer than `p+1` observations are assigned to a predicted regime, the naive estimator is treated as unidentified for that regime in that replication and excluded from coverage — this affects only the most extreme misclassification cells; (ii) the naive estimator does not include any selection-on-prediction correction, which is the primary mechanism the paper compares against.
